Research Interests

Genome organization and regulation

a.) Epigenetics and genomics

The process of epigenetic inheritance includes DNA methylation, post translational modification of histones and chromatin organization and plays an important role in controlling the functional outcomes of DNA sequences. Using diverse sequencing platforms such as ChiP-Seq, RNA-Seq, Bisulfite Sequencing on the Illumina Nova-seq, ONT (Oxford Nanopore Technology) we investigate the modifications on DNA and RNA bases, and DNA encoded regulatory mechanisms. We also harness the power of the large numbers of existing datasets in public repositories to perform meta-analyses and develop new hypothesis on how the genome is regulated.

b) Simple Sequence Repeats

Microsatellites play an important, and as yet unexplored, role in genome regulatory functions. Changes in repeat number and composition in coding regions are associated with many disorders in humans but their role in genome organization is not well understood. Our goal is to understand how microsatellites are conserved and their contribution to the evolution of species complexity.

Population Genetics

India has a large population diversity and a huge untapped potential to understand the genetic basis of health and disease. Our team aims to help generate, manage and analyze quality datasets of population specific genetic samples. We will explore genome diversity to assess the genetic variance in Indian populations and identify the genetic basis for population specific phenotypic expression and disease-associated alleles using Whole Genome Sequencing, Whole Exome Sequencing and SNP indel based studies.

De novo genome assembly

Genome information is crucial for understanding conserved gene regulatory mechanisms and aspects of body design. GRIDS is invested in reconstructing genomes of useful flora and fauna to generate reference genomes using super scaffolding, genome annotation, homology based and gene prediction methods.

Tools and Database Development

Technological advances have led to the generation of unprecedented amounts of biological data from high-through experiments in the last couple of decades which is not easy to access, manage or analyse manually. We work towards solving these problems via computational methods (using C++, Python and R) and develop user friendly and interactive databases for biologists. We also explore new ways to visualize complex data and create novel GUI-based tools for enabling biological data analysis.

Institutional Projects

A large number of projects at the CCMB are focused on dissecting the mechanisms of epigenetic regulation using Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) technologies. The team at GRIDS employs computational and bioinformatics-based approaches to perform the large-scale data analysis that these projects entail. NGS project management including technical and experimental design of the projects, quality control checks, and data management and tracking are provided for a seamless end-to-end service. We also aim to provide bench biologists better access to computational solutions by developing user friendly and interactive tools and databases for data visualization and analysis.
